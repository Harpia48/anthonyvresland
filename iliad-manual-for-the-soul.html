The Iliad is not a relic. It is a current.

Homer does not describe the world. He reorients it.

His poetry is not entertainment. It is initiation.

For centuries, scholars have circled Homer like vultures over a feast they refuse to taste. Did he exist? Was he one or many? Blind or seer? Warrior or bard?

These questions are not wrong — but they are secondary. To fixate on them is to study the ink and miss the fire. To count the syllables and deafen oneself to the song.

The Modern Mistake

For centuries we have approached the Iliad as tourists.

We walk among the ruins of Troy. We count the ships. We debate whether the war truly happened. We analyze tactics, armor, alliances, and kings.

Yet a troubling question remains.

If the Iliad is merely the story of a war, why did it become the foundation of Greek civilization?

The Greeks did not educate their children through military manuals.

They educated them through Homer.

Why?

Because Homer was teaching something far more important than history.

He was teaching the structure of human existence itself.

The Iliad survived not because it preserved the memory of Troy, but because it preserved a map of the human condition. The modern reader sees a battlefield. The ancient reader saw himself.

The Descent into Bios

The Iliad begins with a soul already immersed in matter.

Its heroes are not merely men.

They are living manifestations of forces that every human being encounters: anger, pride, desire, ambition, loyalty, wisdom, fear, and duty.

The battlefield is therefore not merely Troy.

It is Bios itself. The realm of becoming. The realm into which every soul descends.

Every human life unfolds within this field. Every existence contains conflict, temptation, attachment, loss, victory, grief, love, and obligation.

The genius of Homer is that he transforms these invisible realities into visible drama.

What appears to be war is often psychology. What appears to be history is often metaphysics. What appears to be Troy is often the human soul.

Μῆνιν ἄειδε, θεά… Sing, Goddess, of the rage…
Speak. Listen. Awaken.

The blind poet is waiting. The lyre is tuned.
And in that rage — your own awakening begins.

This is not ridiculous. It is revolutionary. And it is yours.
